# Story Shaping Reference

This reference distills Chapter 4 of *Storytelling with You* into reusable rules for turning a storyboard into a story path.

## Core principle

After audience, message, and storyboard, there is still one more planning move:

- decide whether the communication should stay linear,
- become story-shaped,
- or use a hybrid.

The purpose is not performance. The purpose is to create a path that makes the real stakes visible for the audience.

## 1. Linear path vs story path

The default business structure is often linear:

- background,
- process,
- findings,
- recommendation.

This is easy to produce because it mirrors how the work happened.

The problem:

- it can be speaker-centered,
- flat,
- and weak on memorability and action.

Story changes the organizing logic from chronology to audience-relevant tension.

## 2. Use story deliberately, not automatically

Story is often useful when:

- you need to lead people on a journey,
- understanding must build over time,
- attention or action is weak,
- or the decision depends on making tension visible.

Be cautious when:

- the organization strongly expects a rigid format,
- the departure would be rejected before it is heard,
- or the setting is too high-stakes to test a new structure.

Valid outcomes:

- full story path,
- hybrid structure,
- or explicit linear path.

## 3. Identify the audience tension

The tension that shapes the communication is not:

- what frustrated the speaker,
- what felt hard during the project,
- or the raw chronology of the work.

It is the tension that matters to the audience.

Useful questions:

- What is at stake for them?
- What gap exists between current and desired state?
- What issue would their action resolve?
- What unwelcome truth, risk, tradeoff, or opportunity do they need to face?

If the tension is unclear:

- work backward from the desired action,
- compare current vs desired state,
- or talk through it with someone else.

## 4. Choose a story structure

### Plot, twist, ending

Use for simpler communication.

- **Plot**: what is happening?
- **Twist**: what changed, failed, or matters?
- **Ending**: what should happen next?

### Narrative arc

Use when the communication benefits from a fuller journey.

- **Plot**: what context or assumptions does the audience need?
- **Rising action**: how will you surface and build the tension?
- **Climax**: what is the peak insight, risk, or inconvenient truth?
- **Falling action**: what bridge helps people process the peak?
- **Resolution**: what decision, discussion, or action should follow?

Important:

- the arc does not need perfect dramatic symmetry,
- the climax does not need to sit in the middle,
- and the structure should fit the audience and situation.

## 5. Rearrange the storyboard into a path

Take the storyboard content and relocate it into the chosen shape.

Working rules:

- keep the audience's tension visible,
- make transitions explicit,
- remove detail that weakens the path,
- and add back detail only when it strengthens comprehension or trust.

This is where a storyboard becomes a story.

## 6. Separate main narrative from proof

When the audience needs both clarity and credibility, do not force every proof point into the main story.

Split the communication into:

- **main narrative** for attention, meaning, and decision-making,
- **appendix / proof / follow-up** for detail, robustness, backup evidence, or drill-down.

Use this when:

- credibility is still being established,
- the analysis is dense,
- the audience may want optional deep detail,
- or the main path gets muddy under too much evidence.

## 7. Story can exist inside the presentation too

You can also use smaller stories within the overall path:

- to build credibility,
- illustrate a concept,
- make a point memorable,
- connect emotionally,
- or frame a decision.

Only keep them when they serve the message and feel authentic.

## Failure modes

- Retelling the project history just because it is available.
- Using speaker tension instead of audience tension.
- Forcing a dramatic arc where none is needed.
- Confusing story with performance or theatrics.
- Keeping all evidence in the main path and muddying the communication.
- Treating story as mandatory rather than situational.

## Working rule

You are not telling your story. You are forming a story for the audience.
