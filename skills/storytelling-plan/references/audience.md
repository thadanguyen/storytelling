# Audience Planning Reference

This reference distills Chapter 1 of *Storytelling with You* into a reusable planning model.

## Core principle

Communication is not primarily for the speaker. It is for the audience.

The practical implication:

- do not start with "what do I want to say?"
- start with "who needs to hear this, what do they care about, and what will move them to act?"

## The three-part model

| Step | Question | Output |
|---|---|---|
| Prioritize | Who is this really for? | Target audience |
| Assess | What matters to them? | Motivations, resistance, constraints |
| Tailor | How should I communicate? | Framing, detail, format, setup |

## 1. Prioritize your target audience

### Single-person audience

Best case:

- one person commissioned the work,
- one person is the main decision maker,
- or one person has outsized influence.

In that case, plan around:

- what they care about,
- what motivates them,
- what scares them,
- what causes hesitation,
- how they want to be communicated to.

### Mixed audience danger

The default mistake is planning for everyone equally.

That usually leads to:

- broad, diluted framing,
- mismatched detail,
- and no one feeling the message was truly for them.

### Narrowing method

Use this two-step reduction:

1. **Narrow to now**
2. **Identify the decision maker**

`Narrow to now` means:

- ignore everyone who may care later,
- focus on who matters at the current stage of the process.

`Identify the decision maker` means:

- find the person or small subset whose judgment most affects the outcome.

## 2. Working with mixed audiences

When the audience cannot be reduced to one person, use these tactics.

### Recognize and call out differences

- acknowledge that different people in the room need different things,
- set expectations for scope and depth,
- offer follow-up paths for those who need more detail.

### Identify overlap

Ask:

- what common stake unites this group?
- what shared pain, goal, or tension can anchor the discussion?

Use the overlap to frame the conversation.

### Create personas

For large groups, segment into a handful of meaningful categories.

A useful persona includes:

- role or orientation,
- what they care about,
- likely biases,
- likely objections,
- what tends to persuade them.

### Show something relevant to everyone

Useful choices:

- comparison across teams,
- shared metric,
- poll result,
- or common context slide.

The goal is to create a reason for every segment to engage before the message narrows.

### Verbalize to regain attention

When a section is especially relevant to a person or segment, say so directly.

Examples:

- "Finance will want to pay attention to this section."
- "Jane, this next part affects your team's process."

This is attention steering, not decoration.

## 3. Assess the audience

Use these questions as the default probe set:

- What do they care about?
- What drives them?
- What scares them?
- What motivates action?
- What makes them hesitate?
- What do they like or dislike?
- Who influences them?
- How credible are you in their eyes?
- Why are they likely to support you?
- Why are they likely to resist you?
- What biases do they have?
- What constraints bind them?
- How do they define success?

## 4. If the audience is unfamiliar

Reduce guesswork by:

- talking to them directly,
- talking to someone who knows them,
- talking to people similar to them,
- collecting data,
- reading about the person, team, company, or industry.

If you still have to make assumptions:

- label them clearly,
- pressure-test them with colleagues,
- and note which ones could materially change the plan.

## 5. Tailor for the audience

Tailoring is broader than wording.

### General approach

Decide:

- meeting, email, memo, deck, or call,
- length,
- direct answer first vs context first.

### Materials

Decide:

- polished deck vs casual discussion,
- pre-read vs in-room walkthrough,
- slides vs handout,
- big picture vs full detail,
- simple visuals vs heavier data.

### Environment

Decide:

- where to meet,
- when to meet,
- and who else should be involved.

## Tailoring levers

| Lever | Choices |
|---|---|
| Framing | risk, opportunity, upside, competition, urgency, credibility |
| Detail | executive summary, selective detail, full appendix |
| Format | conversation, memo, deck, pre-read, whiteboard |
| Setting | office, conference room, virtual, walk-and-talk |
| Participants | solo, sponsor, expert, influencer, ally |

## Common patterns from the chapter

### Same fact, different framing

A message can be framed as:

- a blind spot,
- a growth opportunity,
- a productivity lever,
- a competitive issue,
- or a credibility risk.

The right frame depends on the audience.

### Competitive framing can motivate action

Some leaders respond less to abstract principle and more to comparison with peers.

If audience analysis shows competition matters, lead with benchmarking.

## Practical output template

Use this shape when producing an audience plan:

### Communication goal
- What should happen after this communication?

### Target audience now
- Who matters at this stage?

### Decision maker / influencers
- Who decides?
- Who can help or block?

### Audience map
- goals
- fears
- motivations
- resistance
- biases
- credibility issues

### Shared overlap
- What unites the room?

### Tailoring choices
- framing
- detail
- format
- setting
- participants

### Assumptions and risks
- What is known?
- What is assumed?
- What must be verified before presenting?

## Failure modes

- Treating "everyone" as the real audience.
- Confusing eventual stakeholders with current stakeholders.
- Assuming others share your preferences.
- Forcing one level of detail on every audience segment.
- Learning about key objections for the first time in the room.

## Working rule

Meet the audience's needs first, and you improve your odds of getting your own needs met.
