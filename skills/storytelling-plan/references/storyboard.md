# Storyboard Planning Reference

This reference distills Chapter 3 of *Storytelling with You* into a reusable planning model.

## Core principle

Do not jump from a clear message to polished slides.

First, turn the message into a **storyboard**:

- a rough content plan,
- a testable structure,
- and a cheap way to rearrange, discard, and improve before production.

## The planning workflow

| Stage | Question | Output |
|---|---|---|
| Brainstorm | What might help land the message? | Candidate content pool |
| Edit | What actually belongs? | Curated material |
| Order | In what sequence should the audience receive it? | Communication path |
| Feedback | Does the rough plan make sense? | Early correction |

## 1. Stay low tech early

The point of low-fidelity planning is not nostalgia. It is decision quality.

Benefits:

- avoids false progress from making slides too soon,
- keeps feedback focused on logic and structure,
- and reduces attachment to weak content.

Working rule:

- keep the plan cheap to change until the structure is defensible.

## 2. Brainstorm broadly

Start with a short, bounded idea-generation pass.

Good brainstorming items include:

- historical or environmental context,
- the problem statement, question, or hypothesis,
- the Big Idea,
- assumptions,
- biases to address,
- data points,
- graphs or other visuals,
- analysis or methodology details,
- process steps,
- examples,
- findings,
- alternative hypotheses,
- options,
- discussion points,
- and recommendations.

Do not judge order too early. The first goal is breadth, not sequence.

## 3. Re-brainstorm from other perspectives

After generating ideas from the speaker's perspective, switch lenses.

Useful perspectives:

- the primary audience,
- a skeptic,
- a decision maker,
- a key influencer,
- a manager or colleague,
- and important audience segments in a mixed room.

This often reveals:

- missing proof,
- likely objections,
- uneven detail,
- and shared overlap that can anchor the communication.

## 4. Edit, group, and discard

After brainstorming, begin curation.

Typical moves:

- group related ideas,
- create section labels,
- add transitions,
- combine overlapping items,
- identify where examples or evidence are needed,
- and cut material that does not support the message.

Use this filter repeatedly:

- "Does this help get the Big Idea across?"

If not, move it to:

- discard,
- hold,
- appendix,
- or follow-up.

Intentional discard is a core strength of storyboarding.

## 5. Storyboard at multiple levels

For longer talks, reports, or projects, storyboard more than once.

Useful zoom levels:

- **macro**: main sections or acts,
- **meso**: section-level flow,
- **micro**: individual slide, paragraph, or example plan.

Working rule:

- validate the whole, then validate the parts.

## 6. Choose where the Big Idea belongs

There is no universal answer.

### Build up to the Big Idea

Use when:

- the audience may disagree,
- your credibility is limited,
- or they need to see the logic before accepting the answer.

### Lead with the Big Idea

Use when:

- rapport is established,
- the audience mainly wants the "So what?",
- time is limited,
- interruption risk is high,
- or likely alignment is already strong.

### Decision rule

Choose ordering based on:

- trust,
- disagreement risk,
- attention constraints,
- and whether the audience values the answer more than the path.

## 7. Seek directional feedback early

Before production, walk someone through the rough plan.

Useful reviewers:

- colleague,
- manager,
- stakeholder,
- sponsor,
- or friendly insider who understands the audience.

Goal:

- validate direction before investing heavily in slides or writing.

Useful framing:

- "This is rough, but here's what I'm thinking."

## 8. Team and remote storyboarding

For team work:

- align on high-level flow first,
- then divide ownership with the shared structure visible.

For remote work:

- use shared documents, whiteboards, slide sorter views, or other movable structures.

The principle is not physical sticky notes. It is shared, low-cost rearrangement.

## Practical output template

Use this shape when producing a storyboard plan:

### Candidate content
- What might belong?

### Storyboard v1
- Main sections
- Key transitions
- Supporting evidence or examples

### Cut / hold / appendix
- What should likely not be in the core flow?

### Ordering choice
- Lead with Big Idea, build up to it, or hybrid
- Why this fits the audience and situation

### Feedback plan
- Who should react to the rough structure before production?

## Failure modes

- Going from Big Idea straight to slides.
- Confusing polished output with good planning.
- Keeping content because it was expensive to make.
- Brainstorming only from the speaker's perspective.
- Treating all relevant information as presentation-worthy.
- Defaulting to one ordering style out of habit.
- Waiting too long to get directional feedback.

## Working rule

The storyboard is where you turn a message into a path the audience can actually follow.
