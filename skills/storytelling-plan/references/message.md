# Message Planning Reference

This reference distills Chapter 2 of *Storytelling with You* into a reusable message-planning model.

## Core principle

Do not start building slides until you can clearly state what you want the audience to do.

The practical implication:

- define the action first,
- express the message in a short spoken form,
- then condense it into one sentence that can guide the rest of the communication.

## The two message tools

| Tool | Question it answers | Output |
|---|---|---|
| 3-minute story | "If I only had a few minutes, what would I say?" | Short spoken story |
| Big Idea | "What is the single point I need them to leave with?" | One-sentence message |

## 1. Start with the action

Before drafting content, clarify:

- what should happen after the communication,
- what decision, discussion, approval, alignment, or behavior change is needed,
- and whether the speaker is informing, guiding, or recommending.

If the desired action is unclear, the message is not ready.

## 2. Form the 3-minute story

Use this structure:

1. **Plot**: the context the audience needs.
2. **Twist**: the new, surprising, or decision-relevant information.
3. **Ending**: the action you want the audience to take.

### Why this matters

- It helps separate the essential from the merely available.
- It creates a version you can say aloud without slides.
- It becomes the bridge between a messy situation and a sharper one-sentence message.

### Working rule

Write the 3-minute story in plain language, then read it aloud and tighten it.

## 3. Compose the Big Idea

The Big Idea is the single sentence that should guide the communication.

It should:

1. **Articulate a point of view**
2. **Convey what is at stake for the audience**
3. **Be a complete single sentence**

## 4. Make the sentence useful

### Articulate a real point of view

Weak:

- generic,
- obvious,
- noncommittal,
- or so broad that no one could disagree.

Stronger:

- specific,
- directional,
- and clear about what the speaker thinks should happen.

### Frame the stakes for the audience

The stakes are not mainly:

- why this matters to the speaker,
- how much work went into it,
- or why the speaker personally cares.

The stakes are:

- what the audience gains if they act,
- what they risk if they do not,
- and why this matters in their world.

### Positive vs negative framing

Choose deliberately:

- **Positive framing**: lead with upside, gain, progress, benefit.
- **Negative framing**: lead with risk, loss, cost, downside.

Use whichever is more likely to create the desired response.

Do not assume negative framing is always stronger. In defensive or emotionally charged situations, it can backfire.

## 5. Use constraints on purpose

The single-sentence limit is useful because it forces prioritization.

When the message will not fit:

- start longer and cut,
- or draft the 3-minute story first and condense from there,
- or build the sentence piece by piece.

Difficulty is diagnostic: if the idea cannot be said clearly in one sentence, the communication probably needs more thinking.

## 6. Big Idea worksheet prompts

Use these prompts when the user is struggling to draft the sentence:

1. Who is the audience?
2. If you had to narrow to one person, who is it?
3. What do they care about?
4. What action do they need to take?
5. What are the benefits if they act?
6. What are the risks if they do not?

Then combine the pieces into one sentence.

## 7. Common failure modes

- Starting with slides instead of a message.
- Naming information to share without naming the action to inform.
- Framing the stakes around the speaker instead of the audience.
- Trying to include too many details or numbers in the core sentence.
- Using a sentence that is technically complete but strategically vague.
- Leaving the recommendation strength undecided.

## 8. Feedback and team use

The message usually improves when discussed with other people.

Useful pressure-test questions:

- Who?
- So what?
- Why now?
- What should happen next?

In a team setting:

1. Have individuals draft separate Big Ideas.
2. Compare them side by side.
3. Discuss differences in framing, boldness, and emphasis.
4. Merge the strongest parts into a master Big Idea.

## Practical output template

Use this shape when producing a message plan:

### Desired action
- What should happen after the communication?

### 3-minute story
- Plot
- Twist
- Ending

### Big Idea
- Single sentence

### Framing choice
- positive or negative
- why this framing fits the audience

### Content filter
- What must stay?
- What should likely be cut?

## Working rule

If a detail does not help get the Big Idea across, it probably does not belong in the core communication.
