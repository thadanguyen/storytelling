# Storytelling Plan

Use this skill when the user is planning communication before creating slides or rehearsing delivery.

This skill covers four linked planning layers:

- **Audience planning**:
  - who the communication is really for,
  - who matters now,
  - who decides,
  - what motivates or blocks the audience,
  - and how to tailor the approach accordingly.
- **Message planning**:
  - what the audience should do,
  - how to express the story in a short spoken form,
  - and how to condense it into a single-sentence Big Idea.
- **Storyboard planning**:
  - what candidate content belongs,
  - what should be cut or held,
  - how to organize and order the communication,
  - and how to pressure-test the plan before production.
- **Story shaping**:
  - whether the communication should stay linear, become story-shaped, or use a hybrid,
  - what tension matters to the audience,
  - how to map the plan to a narrative arc,
  - and when to separate the main story from appendix, proof, or follow-up detail.

Read:

- `references/audience.md` for Chapter 1 audience planning.
- `references/message.md` for Chapter 2 message planning.
- `references/storyboard.md` for Chapter 3 storyboard planning.
- `references/story.md` for Chapter 4 story shaping.

## Quick start

1. Identify the communication context.
2. Name the current audience and desired action.
3. Ask whether the audience is too broad.
4. Narrow to now.
5. Identify the decision maker or highest-leverage subset.
6. Map motivations, fears, resistance, and overlap.
7. Draft the 3-minute story.
8. Condense to a Big Idea.
9. Brainstorm candidate content before committing to an outline.
10. Rearrange, group, and cut using the Big Idea as a filter.
11. Identify the audience tension and decide whether story is the right shape.
12. Map the plan to a story path, hybrid path, or explicit linear path.
13. Separate main narrative from appendix / proof when needed.
14. Tailor framing, detail, format, and meeting setup.
15. Produce a reusable planning output.

## Inputs to collect

Collect or infer:

- what the user is trying to communicate,
- the current situation or decision,
- the audience list,
- the desired action or outcome,
- what is known about key people,
- what material, evidence, or examples might need to be shown,
- whether there are template, cultural, or political constraints on format,
- whether credibility must be established through visible proof or detail,
- what tension likely matters to the audience,
- what assumptions are being made,
- and whether the user needs audience planning, message planning, storyboard planning, or the full stack.

Ask only for missing details that materially change the output.

## Default workflow

### 1) Define the communication moment

Clarify:

- what is being presented,
- why now,
- what success looks like,
- and what action should happen after the communication.

If the user is vague, force specificity:

- decision,
- alignment,
- approval,
- discussion,
- behavior change,
- or simple awareness.

### 2) Prioritize the audience

Start with the full audience list, then reduce it.

Use two questions:

1. Who matters **now**, at this stage?
2. Who actually decides or most influences the outcome?

If the user names a large mixed audience, do not accept that at face value. Push toward:

- one person,
- one deciding subgroup,
- or a small set of distinct personas.

### 3) Assess the audience

Use `references/audience.md` to map:

- goals,
- fears,
- incentives,
- hesitations,
- biases,
- influencers,
- credibility issues,
- support and resistance.

Prefer concrete observations over generic labels.

Weak:

- "senior leader"

Stronger:

- "cost-focused CFO who wants direct answers, low tolerance for ambiguity, and likely pushes on downside risk"

### 4) Handle mixed audiences deliberately

When the audience cannot be narrowed to one person:

- identify overlap,
- identify meaningful segments,
- decide what to keep high level,
- decide who needs pre-briefing or follow-up,
- and make tradeoffs explicit.

Do not try to optimize every section for every person at once.

### 5) Craft the message

Use `references/message.md`.

First, force clarity on the action:

- what should happen after the communication,
- what should the audience discuss, decide, approve, reject, or understand,
- and whether the user is being neutral or making a recommendation.

Then produce two message forms:

- a **3-minute story** with:
  - plot,
  - twist,
  - ending.
- a **Big Idea** that:
  - states a point of view,
  - conveys what is at stake for the audience,
  - and is a complete single sentence.

If the sentence is vague, overloaded, or speaker-centered, revise it.

### 6) Storyboard the communication path

Use `references/storyboard.md`.

Do not jump from Big Idea straight to polished outline or slides.

First, run a **divergent pass**:

- brainstorm candidate content,
- include facts, examples, visuals, objections, options, assumptions, and recommendations,
- and re-brainstorm from key audience perspectives when useful.

Then run a **convergent pass**:

- group related ideas,
- identify transitions,
- create a discard / hold pile,
- and keep asking:
  - "Does this help get the Big Idea across?"

If the user already has plenty of raw material, skip straight to curation and ordering.

### 7) Decide whether to shape with story

Use `references/story.md`.

Do not assume story is always the answer.

Check:

- whether the audience needs to be led on a journey,
- whether attention or action is weak enough that shape matters,
- whether real audience-relevant tension exists,
- whether the culture or format strongly resists a story-first structure,
- and whether the situation is too high-stakes to experiment with a new structure.

Possible outcomes:

- **story path**,
- **hybrid path**,
- or **explicit linear path**.

If story is a poor fit, say so and keep the plan direct.

### 8) Identify the audience tension

Do not use the speaker's pain or project chronology as the source of story.

Identify:

- what is at stake for the audience,
- what gap exists between current and desired state,
- what issue their action would resolve,
- what unwelcome truth, risk, tradeoff, or opportunity they need to confront,
- and what form of tension is strong enough to organize the communication.

If the tension is unclear:

- work backward from the desired action,
- compare current vs desired state,
- or talk it through with someone else.

### 9) Form the story path

If story is appropriate, rearrange the storyboard into a path.

Choose a structure that fits the situation:

- **plot, twist, ending** for simpler communication,
- **narrative arc** for a fuller journey:
  - plot,
  - rising action,
  - climax,
  - falling action,
  - resolution,
- or a hybrid that uses story shape without strict dramatic symmetry.

Important rules:

- the tension must belong to the audience, not the presenter,
- the climax does not need to be in the middle,
- and the goal is not theater; the goal is to make the real stakes visible.

### 10) Decide the ordering strategy

Do not assume the Big Idea always belongs at the start.

Choose deliberately between:

- **lead with the Big Idea**,
- **build up to it linearly**,
- or a hybrid structure.

Use these signals:

- audience credibility and trust,
- likelihood of disagreement,
- time pressure,
- interruption risk,
- whether the audience cares more about the answer or the reasoning path,
- and whether leading with tension or leading with resolution will better hold attention.

### 11) Separate main story from proof

If the audience needs both clarity and trust, do not force every detail into the main path.

Consider splitting the communication into:

- **main narrative**:
  - the path that carries attention, meaning, and decision-making,
- **appendix / proof / follow-up**:
  - methods,
  - backup evidence,
  - detailed breakdowns,
  - alternative paths,
  - or material needed to establish robustness.

Use this especially when:

- credibility is still being established,
- the work is analytically dense,
- the audience may want optional drill-down,
- or the story becomes muddy when every proof point stays in the core flow.

### 12) Tailor the communication

At minimum, decide:

- framing,
- level of detail,
- format,
- setting,
- timing,
- and whether to involve supporters or influencers.

Tailoring should reflect the audience analysis, not the speaker's default preferences.

### 13) Pressure-test assumptions and the rough plan

If important audience or message facts are uncertain:

- label them as assumptions,
- estimate how risky they are,
- and note what to verify before the communication.

Also pressure-test:

- whether the message names a real action,
- whether the stakes are framed for the audience rather than the speaker,
- whether the Big Idea is memorable enough to guide content selection,
- whether the storyboard leaves out anything the audience must see to believe the message,
- whether the chosen path is overly chronological simply because that is how the work happened,
- whether the tension is real, audience-centered, and strong enough to shape the communication,
- whether the story path and appendix split are doing enough to balance clarity with credibility,
- and whether directional feedback should be gathered before content production starts.

## Default output

Unless the user asks otherwise, produce:

1. `Communication goal`
2. `Target audience now`
3. `Decision maker / key influencers`
4. `Audience map`
5. `Shared overlap`
6. `3-minute story`
7. `Big Idea`
8. `Storyboard v1`
9. `Audience tension`
10. `Story path`
11. `Main story vs appendix / proof`
12. `Cut / hold / appendix candidates`
13. `Ordering choice`
14. `Tailoring choices`
15. `Risks and assumptions`
16. `Recommended next move`

## Behavior rules

- Start with the audience and message, not the slides.
- Treat planning as four layers: audience, message, storyboard, then story shape.
- Prefer narrowing over broadening.
- Separate `known` from `assumed`.
- Treat mixed audiences as segmentable, not hopeless.
- Optimize for action, not for exhaustive description.
- Make the desired action explicit before drafting content.
- Separate divergent planning from convergent planning.
- Keep planning low-fidelity until the structure is defensible.
- Use the Big Idea as a litmus test for what belongs.
- Treat discard as a core planning move, not as failure.
- Do not default to chronology simply because that is how the work happened.
- Form a story for the audience rather than recounting the speaker's process.
- Distinguish speaker tension from audience tension.
- Use story to illuminate real stakes, not to manufacture drama.
- Story can be a full arc, a simpler plot-twist-ending, a hybrid, or not the right move at all.
- Choose Big Idea placement intentionally rather than by habit.
- Separate the main narrative from supporting proof when that improves both clarity and trust.
- Seek directional feedback before production feedback when possible.
- If the user wants a note in the vault, follow `notes_style.md` and local folder conventions after the audience plan is clear.
